When you talk to something to someone in person, you sort of know who it is, not to mention that he's not a robot. But it's not like that online.
This talk and the website features ridiculous comics. drops melodramatic expressions:
  • "Liberated Trust"
  • "Freedom of Speech 2.0"
  • "Democracy2.0"
We understand that:
  • we can already say whatever we want to online
  • there's spam, lies, or worse..

We're cautious online.. might be a scam, a bot, a troll, a liar..
If you're not, how can the rest of us know?

Does it really matter that you can say whatever you want online if we can't know that you're one of us?

If you have a good reputation (with others), and someone you know has a good reputation with you, how can you pass that along? Not just vouch that he's a good Uber driver, Reddit poster, Airbnb renter, or Ebay seller, but identify him and identify yourself regardless of a service or an account.

As you explore this offering, keep in mind that

  1. Trusted society grounded by our existing human relationships
  2. Decentralized organization

What happened..
When you scan phones:
  • You reference the other person's public key
  • You sign a statement vouching for his humanity and identity using your own private crypto identity key
    • Your private identity key always stays on your phone (unless you export it for use with other software - know what you're doing)
  • You publish that signed statement in a location where everything your key has signed can be found.
When you create a delegate, you:
  • Create a new, disposable public/private key pair
  • Sign that this key represents you on that service
  • Publish that statement where it can be found and verified using your key's token
  • Securely transmit that key pair to the service
Complete Cryptographic Digital Signature Chain
  • from your key to all the whitelisted content you see.
Show statements and keys on the apps.
  • Statements
  • Keys
  • Raw, interpreted, QR, text
  • Link to published statements
    That's where both apps load data from (and only)
All activities on the apps sign and publish statements
  • portable, verifiable by anything as having been done by that key
Both apps get all of their content from those same published statements.
  • Nerdster and the Identity Layer are not connected, have no special access.
Heterogeneous:

Anyone can make a different, better services leveraging the existing signed, trusted, content, but better..

  • Better conflict detection or identity Network,
  • better UI, richer abilities

... and that can fragment even further with competing services playing off each other's improvements.
Can't do that with our Facebook, X, Insta content..

Your identity is your crypto key. That's the paradigm. That's all.

The Internet can evolve, take it from there, wherever that may go..
  • Identity App: an example of an app that can contribute to our decentralized identity network
  • Nerdster: an example of a service that leverages that network and lets us browse and create authentic, whitelisted content
  • You can use any tech to sign with it, issue delegates, claim and revoke old keys, vouch for others
  • Folks can be their own identities on any service, not an account at a particular service
  • Folks can follow or otherwise reference identities of actual people instead of accounts at a particular service
